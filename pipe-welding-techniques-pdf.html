10. how to weld carbon steel pipe in 1G position?​


Answer:

Welding carbon steel pipe in the 1G position can be done using several welding processes, including Shielded Metal Arc Welding (SMAW), Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW), Gas Metal Arc Welding (GMAW), and Flux-Cored Arc Welding (FCAW).

Here are the general steps for welding carbon steel pipe in the 1G position using SMAW:

Prepare the pipe - Cut the pipe to the desired length and bevel the ends to create a groove for the weld. Make sure that the bevel angle and root gap are according to the welding procedure specification (WPS).

Set up the equipment - Set up the welding machine, select the appropriate welding rod, and adjust the current and polarity settings.

Position the pipe - Position the pipe in the 1G position, which means it is lying horizontally, and the weld is in a vertical direction.

Tack weld - Tack weld the two ends of the pipe to hold them in place. Ensure that the tack welds are aligned with the beveled edges.

Weld the root pass - Start welding the root pass, making sure to maintain a consistent speed and angle. Use the stringer technique to deposit the weld metal into the root gap. The root pass should penetrate the full thickness of the pipe, and the reinforcement should be flush with the surface of the pipe.

Fill and cap passes - After completing the root pass, continue with the fill and cap passes, making sure to overlap the previous pass slightly. Use the weave technique to deposit the weld metal, and ensure that the final weld bead is smooth and free of porosity, cracks, or other defects.

Clean and inspect - Clean the weld area to remove any slag or spatter, and inspect the weld for any defects. If any defects are found, repair them before proceeding.

By following these steps and adhering to the welding procedure specification (WPS), you can successfully weld carbon steel pipe in the 1G position using SMAW.

FILED UNDER: AIRCRAFT WELDING

Multiple Pass Welding

Grove and fillet welds in heavy metals often require the deposit of a number of beads to complete a weld. It is important that the beads be deposited in a predetermined sequence to produce the soundest welds with the best proportions. The number of beads is determined by the thickness of the metal being welded.

Plates from 1⁄8-inch to ¼-inch can be welded in one pass, but they should be tacked at intervals to keep them aligned. Any weld on a plate thicker than ¼-inch should have the edges beveled and multiple passes.
