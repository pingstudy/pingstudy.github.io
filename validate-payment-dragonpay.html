Tanggol replied that the same had been condoned by his father as evidenced by a notation at the back of his check payment for the P300,000.00 reading: "In full payment of the loan". Will this be a valid defense in an action for collection? Explain.


The validity of Tanggol's defense of condonation depends on whether or not there was a valid condonation made by his father before he died. Condonation is the act of forgiving a debt and extinguishing it, and it must be made voluntarily by the creditor with the intention of releasing the debtor from the obligation of the debt.

In this case, the notation on the back of the check payment for P300,000.00 may be considered evidence of the father's intention to condone the remaining P200,000.00 debt. However, it is not conclusive evidence, and it can be rebutted by other evidence showing that the father did not intend to forgive the entire debt.

The administrator of the estate can argue that the notation on the check only refers to the amount of P300,000.00 and does not necessarily mean that the entire debt has been condoned. The father could have simply intended to acknowledge the partial payment made by Tanggol.

Therefore, Tanggol's defense of condonation may not be valid in an action for collection if it is proven that there was no intention to forgive the entire debt, and the remaining balance of P200,000.00 still needs to be paid.

11. 1. Differentiate the types of validity (Content validity, Construct Validity and Criterion-related Validity) 2. Compare structured and unstructured questionnaire.


Answer:

1. Validity is the extent to which a measure or assessment tool accurately measures what it intends to measure. There are three types of validity:

- Content validity: This refers to the degree to which a measurement tool represents all aspects of the construct being measured. It evaluates the comprehensiveness and representativeness of the items included in the measure. Content validity is often established through expert judgment, and the items are usually based on a careful review of the literature or consultation with subject matter experts.

- Construct validity: This refers to the degree to which a measurement tool accurately measures the theoretical construct it is intended to measure. It involves demonstrating that the measure correlates with other measures or outcomes in a manner consistent with theoretical predictions. This type of validity is usually established through factor analysis, convergent validity, and discriminant validity.

- Criterion-related validity: This refers to the degree to which a measurement tool accurately predicts or correlates with a criterion measure. It involves establishing a correlation between the measure being tested and a criterion measure that is already known to be valid. Criterion-related validity can be established through concurrent validity, which involves comparing the results of the measure being tested with the criterion measure at the same time, or through predictive validity, which involves comparing the results of the measure being tested with the criterion measure at a future point in time.

2. A questionnaire is a research instrument used to collect data from participants in a standardized and structured way. Questionnaires can be structured or unstructured:

- Structured questionnaire: This type of questionnaire has a fixed set of questions with predetermined response options, which are usually closed-ended questions (yes/no, multiple choice, rating scales, etc.). Structured questionnaires are easier to analyze and compare across participants, and they provide more reliable and objective data.

- Unstructured questionnaire: This type of questionnaire is more flexible and open-ended, allowing participants to respond in their own words without predetermined response options. Unstructured questionnaires are often used in qualitative research to explore participants' experiences, perceptions, and attitudes in more depth. They are more subjective and difficult to analyze, but they provide richer and more detailed data.

30. Operationalize the five ways of establishing validity in measurement by giving each two (2) exampleWays of Establishing Validity 1. Face Validity 2. Content Validity3. Predictive Validity4. Construct Validity5. Convergent Validityexample1.2.3.4.5.​


Validity tells you how accurately a method measures something. The types of validity mentioned are construct, content, face, criterion, convergent, and prediction validity that will be elaborated below.

Construct validity

Example:

We cannot directly measure "depression" as an objective, observable thing. However, based on current psychology theory and research, we are able to quantify depression using a range of signs and symptoms, such as low energy and self-confidence.

Content validity

Example:

An end-of-semester algebra test is created by a math instructor for her class. Every type of algebra that was covered in class should be covered on the test. If particular forms of algebra are omitted, the findings could not be a reliable reflection of how well pupils comprehend the material. Likewise, if she adds questions that have nothing to do with algebra, the findings are no longer a reliable indicator of algebra proficiency.

Face validity

Example:

You design a poll to gauge how regularly people practice their eating habits. You look over the survey items, which include inquiries regarding each day's meals as well as any between-meal snacks. The survey appears to be a good depiction of what you want to test on the surface, therefore you value its high face validity.

Criterion validity

Example:

A university professor develops a brand-new exam to gauge candidates' proficiency in English writing. She locates an existing test that is thought to be a valid measurement of English writing ability and analyzes the results when the same set of students take both tests in order to determine how effectively the test actually measures students' writing skill. The new test has high criterion validity when the results are strikingly comparable.

Convergent validity

Example:

Use both participant observation and a survey as measurements. It is clear that they are measuring the same construct if the results of your two measurements are comparable (i.e., they converge).

Predictive validity

Example:

If people who score highly on an honesty test later demonstrate their honesty by their conduct, the exam has predictive validity.
