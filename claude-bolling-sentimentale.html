What are the similarities of Claude Debussy and Claude Monet?

The impressionist composers are often linked to painters from the same period and with a similar artistic agenda. The music of Debussy is full of symbolism and uses a variety of scale forms and compositional devices that in some ways get closer to improvisation than composers from lets say the earlier Germanic tradition . Debussy loved to find appropriate ways to develop his works which seemed to be on a path of allowing inspiration to be part of the developmental process and not just a regular means of development technique as found in the classical sonata form ( for example) . To compare and link Debussy with Monet is not an easy task as it requires some generalisation . Monet had a love for painting nature scenes along with portraits and general scenery . The standout feature as it occurs to me would be linked to how an impressionist sees and uses light and shade in their pictures . In traditional painting much like photography , an artist would typically depict how light falls on a subject to be as close to photgraphically accurate as possible . In impressionist art , the artist often exaggerates this effect , as by doing so they give more to the viewers imagination , and therby increasing the artistic effect when done tastefully . I am sure this is not the last point that could be made regarding this but its the one point that stands out for me personally . Detail on a photographic level was not the goal .Now back to Dbeussy , he lived by creating an imaginary landscape , and made comments about this way of viewing the world and composing to meet this inner desire .So for me the process of both great artistes is actually not dissimilar despite their artforms being different . 18. what is the most essential similarities of claude debussy and claude monet​


By the time Claude Monet was born in 1840, the Industrial Revolution had already turned what were formally rural market towns, particularly in the north of France, into factory and mining wastelands. These we have come to know through the novels of Émile Zola. The suburbs of large towns were also now expanded and darkened by manufacturing, sometimes forming rings around urban areas like the defensive moats that had once encircled these same towns in medieval times.

With this came a growing sense of separation from Nature.

The great impulse to return to Nature, which came with the Romantic movement at the end of the eighteenth century, was intensified in the mid-nineteenth century by a growing awareness of the dehumanisation and impoverishment that was a consequence of industrialisation.

The awareness of the need for Nature’s healing powers was made more acute. Elements, essential to well-being, were being eliminated in the smoke, iron, steam and noise of the dark satanic mills. This fear was manifested in all branches of the arts.
