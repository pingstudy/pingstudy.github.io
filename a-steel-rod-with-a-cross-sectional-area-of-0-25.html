2. a steel rod having a length of 1m has a cross sectional area of 100sq.mm. it is subjected to an axial tensile force of 27KN .modulus of elasticity is 200,000MPa. what is the deformation of the steel bar?​


Answer:

The deformation δ[tex]=0.00135m[/tex]

Step-by-step explanation:

Step 1: To get the deformation of the steel bar, the formula for stress and strain is used.

  δ[tex]=\frac{FL}{AE}[/tex]

  it is derived from the formula σ[tex]=\frac{F}{A}[/tex], ε=δ/L and E= σ/ε

  where:

  δ = strain (or the deformation in [tex]m[/tex])

  F = force (in [tex]N[/tex])

  L = length (of steel rod in [tex]m[/tex])

  A = area (of steel rod [tex]m^{2}[/tex])

  E = modulus of elasticity

  Given:

  [tex]L=1m[/tex]

  [tex]A=100mm^{2}[/tex]

  [tex]E=200000MPa[/tex]

  [tex]F=27kN[/tex]

  Required:

  δ = ?

Step 2: Convert the value of area to [tex]m^{2}[/tex]

  [tex]A=100mm^{2} (\frac{1m}{1000mm} )^{2}=0.0001m^{2}[/tex]

Step 3: Solve for the value of δ

  δ[tex]=\frac{FL}{AE}[/tex]

  δ[tex]=\frac{(27kN)(1m)}{(0.0001m^{2})(200,000MPa)}}[/tex]

  δ[tex]=\frac{(27kN)(1m)}{(0.0001m^{2})(200,000M\frac{N}{m^{2} } )}}[/tex]

  δ[tex]=0.00135m[/tex]

Note: After applying 27kN force to a 1m steel rod having 100[tex]0.0001m^{2}[/tex] section area, it is elongated by 0.00135m.

Modulus of Elasticity, commonly known as Elastic Modulus or Modulus, measures the elasticity of a substance. Elastic modulus assesses a material's resistance to non-permanent, or elastic, deformation.

Young's Modulus (or Elastic Modulus) is essentially a material's stiffness. In other terms, it is the material's pliability or extensibility. To be more precise, the physical and numerical values are calculated as follows: Young's Modulus = Strain/Stress.

23. Carly places one end of a steel bar in a Bunsen flame and the other end in an ice cube. By what factor is the rate of heat flow changed when the bar's cross-sectional area is doubled?​


Answer:

Doubled

Explanation:

The formula for conduction is given by:

Q = kA∆T/t

where: Q = rate of heat transfered

A = cross-sectional area of the material

∆T = Change in temperature

k = Thermal conductivity of the material

t = thickness of the material

The cross-sectional area is directly proportional to the rate of heat transfered, so when the area is doubled, the rate of heat flow or heat transfered is also doubled.

Young's modulus:

[tex]\boxed{E=\dfrac{F/A}{\Delta L/L}}[/tex]

where

E = Young's modulus (stress over strain)

F = force

A = area

ΔL = the change in length

L = the original length
