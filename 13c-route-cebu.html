12. There are two routes Sally can take to travel home from school. Route A is 800 m. Route B is 1 km. What is the percentage decrease in distance if Sally took route A instead of route B?


Answer:

To find the percentage decrease in distance if Sally took route A instead of route B, we first need to calculate the difference between the distances of the two routes:

1 km = 1000 m

So, the difference in distance between Route A and Route B is:

1000 m - 800 m = 200 m

Next, we need to find the percentage decrease from Route B to Route A, which can be calculated using the formula:

% decrease = (difference / original value) x 100%

In this case, the original value is the distance of Route B (1000 m), and the difference is 200 m. Substituting these values into the formula, we get:

% decrease = (200 / 1000) x 100%

% decrease = 0.2 x 100%

% decrease = 20%

Therefore, if Sally took Route A instead of Route B, she would be traveling 20% less distance.

13. What are two advantages of static routing over dynamic routing?


Answer:

Static routing requires very little knowledge of the network for correct implementation. Static routing uses fewer router resources than dynamic routing. Static routing is relatively easy to configure for large networks. Static routing is more secure because it does not advertise over the network.

21. A gas measures 100mL at 26C and 1atm. What will be its volume at 13C and 0.5atm?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

To convert the temperature from degree Celsius to kelvin, add 273 to the temperature expressed in degree Celsius.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & P_1 = \text{1 atm} \\ & V_1 = \text{100 mL} \\ & T_1 = 26^{\circ}\text{C} = \text{299 K} \\ & P_2 = \text{0.5 atm} \\ & T_2 = 13^{\circ}\text{C} = \text{286 K} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the final volume using combined gas law.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \frac{P_1V_1}{T_1} & = \frac{P_2V_2}{T_2} \\ V_2P_2T_1 & = V_1P_1T_2 \\ \frac{V_2P_2T_1}{P_2T_1} & = \frac{V_1P_1T_2}{P_2T_1} \\ V_2 & = \frac{V_1P_1T_2}{P_2T_1} \\ & = \frac{(\text{100 mL})(\text{1 atm})(\text{286 K})}{(\text{0.5 atm})(\text{299 K})} \\ & = \text{191.304 mL} \\ & \approx \boxed{\text{190 mL}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the volume of the gas at 13°C and 0.5 atm is 190 mL.
