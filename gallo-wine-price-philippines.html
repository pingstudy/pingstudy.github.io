19. Can misa de gallo boost the faith of people


Misa de gallo (pronounced MEE-sah day GAH-yoh) means "rooster's mass" in Spanish. Both the Spanish- andPortuguese-speaking peoples of the world refer to Midnight Mass on Christmas Eve as the rooster's mass. ThePortuguese term for "rooster's mass," missado galo, closely resembles its Spanish cousin. 

This curious name for Midnight Mass comes from a bit of old European folklore. According to a traditional tale Jesuswas born at the stroke of midnight. The task of announcing this miraculous event fell to the roosters. The first roosterfluttered to the roof of the stable and proclaimed in a human voice, "Christ is born!" The second followed, crying out,"In Bethlehem!" Since the rooster was the first creature to call humankind to worship on the eve of Jesus' birth, peoplethroughout the Spanish- and Portuguese-speaking worlds honor the animal by referring to Midnight Mass onChristmas Eve as the "rooster's mass." 

Perhaps elements of this legend inspired the scheduling of Midnight Mass itself. Since early medieval times RomanCatholic priests have celebrated three Christmas masses. Rules dating back to the fifth century A . D . ordained thatthe first Christmas mass be celebrated ad gallicantum, that is, when the rooster crows (see also Plygain). Fewroosters crow as early as midnight. Instead, the belief that Jesus was born at midnight determined the hour at whichthe first mass was held. 

20. Misa de Gallo then and now?​


Answer:

"Misa de Gallo" is a Spanish phrase that translates to "Rooster's Mass" in English. It refers to the Catholic tradition of attending Mass at dawn, usually at around 4:00 or 5:00 a.m., during the nine days before Christmas Eve in the Philippines.

The tradition of Misa de Gallo in the Philippines dates back to the Spanish colonial period, when the Spanish friars introduced Catholicism to the country. The practice was a way to encourage the newly converted Filipinos to attend church and celebrate the birth of Jesus Christ.

Today, Misa de Gallo remains an important part of Filipino Christmas traditions. Many Filipinos still wake up early to attend the early morning Masses, which are usually held outdoors or in temporary chapels called "simbang gabi" (night Mass) or "simbang araw" (day Mass) in different parts of the country.

However, there have been some changes to the way Misa de Gallo is celebrated in the modern era. For instance, some churches now offer Masses at different times of the day to accommodate people's schedules, and some Masses are also celebrated online due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Despite these changes, the tradition of Misa de Gallo continues to be an important part of the Christmas season for many Filipinos.
