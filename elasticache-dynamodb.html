5. Water has seen an increase in demand 8% this summer, while the price has decreased 12%A. 1.5 inelasticB. 1.5 elasticC. .67 inelasticD. .67 elastic


The water price (D) 0.67 inelastic.

Explanation :

The elasticity of demand refers to the extent to which demand can influence or respond to price changes. Price is the most commonly used economic factor when determining elasticity.

Inelastic is a condition in which a static quantity of goods or services changes in price. Inelastic demand means that when the price of a good or service rises, consumers' buying habits will remain the same, and when prices fall, consumers' buying habits will also remain unchanged.

Water is an example of an inelastic product because there is nothing that can limit it at this time, no matter the price increase or decrease, the demand for water will always be stable so it is classified as an inelastic product. A price elasticity of 0.67 implies that if the price increases by 1%, the quantity demanded will decrease by 1%. In such a scenario, demand is considered to be price inelastic.

7. A compound dynamo supplies a load of 20 kw at 400 v through a feeder having a resistance equal to 0.1 ohm. the shunt and series field resistances are 85 ohms and 0.06 ohm, respectively. determine the total electromotive force when the generator is connected short-shunt. assume a brush contact drop of 1 v per brush and an armature resistance of 0.1 ohm.


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Solving for the load current

[tex]I_{\text{L}} = \dfrac{P_{\text{L}}}{V_{\text{L}}}[/tex]

[tex]I_{\text{L}} = \dfrac{\text{20000 W}}{\text{400 V}}[/tex]

[tex]I_{\text{L}} = \text{50 A}[/tex]

Solving for the shunt field current

[tex]I_{\text{sh}} = \dfrac{V + I_{\text{L}}(R_{\text{feeder}} + R_\text{se})}{R_{\text{sh}}}[/tex]

[tex]I_{\text{sh}} = \dfrac{\text{400 V} + (\text{50 A})(\text{0.1 ohm + 0.06 ohm})}{\text{85 ohm}}[/tex]

[tex]I_{\text{sh}} = \text{4.8 A}[/tex]

Solving for the armature current

[tex]I_{\text{a}} = I_{\text{L}} + I_{\text{sh}}[/tex]

[tex]I_{\text{a}} = \text{50 A + 4.8 A}[/tex]

[tex]I_{\text{a}} = \text{54.8 A}[/tex]

Solving for the generated emf

[tex]E = V + I_{\text{L}}(R_{\text{feeder}} + R_{\text{se}}) + I_{\text{a}}R_{\text{a}} + V_{\text{brush}}[/tex]

[tex]E = \text{400 V + (50 A)(0.1 ohm + 0.06 ohm) + (54.8 A)(0.1 ohm) + 2(1 V)}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{E = \text{415.48 V}}[/tex]

Therefore, the generated emf is 415.48 V.

18. When was direct-current (dc) dynamo invented?


When was direct-current (dc) dynamo invented?

It was 1827 when the direct-current dynamo was being experimented on by a scientist.

Anyos Jedlik, a Hungarian, began experimenting with electromagnetic rotating devices such as dynamo, which he named electromagnetic self-rotors, in 1827, independently of Faraday. Both the stationary and spinning portions of the single-pole electric starter prototype were electromagnetic.

He conceived the dynamo concept in 1856, six years before Siemens and Wheatstone, but he did not patent it since he believed he was not the first to realize it. His dynamo employed two electromagnets positioned opposite each other to produce the magnetic field surrounding the rotor instead of permanent magnets. It was also the discovery of the dynamo self-excitation, which superseded permanent magnet designs.
