14. 1. “Chicago” In "Chicago," Carl Sandburg describes the city as a "bold slugger," "a savage," a "young man," and "an ignorant fighter." What do all these descriptions have in common? How do they relate to Chicago as a rapidly growing city at the start of the twentieth century? 2. “Grass” As Carl Sandburg's "Grass" ends, the grass again calls to be allowed to work. As readers see in the poem, the work of the grass is to cover the evidence of the tragic events that happen on battlefields so that people no longer recognize these places as battlefields. Is the work of the grass positive or negative? Why? What does it mean that the poem ends with the grass repeating its demand to be allowed to work? 3. “The Road Not Taken” Consider the ambiguity of the poem's final line. The speaker imagines himself in the future stating that his decision to take the road less traveled has made all the difference. Is this necessarily a positive statement or an endorsement of his choice? How might the speaker's statement be interpreted as less than positive? 4. “After Apple Picking” At the conclusion of "After Apple-Picking," the speaker is thinking about his upcoming sleep and wondering if it will be like the hibernation of the woodchuck or "just some human sleep." What is one possible symbolic interpretation of the speaker's sleep? How does interpreting the speaker's sleep as a symbol change the meaning of the rest of the poem?


Story Analysis

1. Chicago

Bold slugger, a savage, a young man, and an ignorant fighter are descriptions of people living in one country particularly in Chicago. Some people of Chicago are like bold slugger since they can be as tough as their punches. They are those who are well adjusted and can be very tough. The second group are like savages who are like monsters. They are the ones who uses their masculinity just to undermine others. There are also groups of people that are like young men who seem too innocent. And the last group are like  ignorant fighters who do not know how to fight and where to begin. Chicago was once like these groups of people during the twentieth century. The city was filled with different groups of people just like them who have different ways of approaching life challenges.

2. Grass

The work of the grass can be both positive and negative. Covering the evidence of tragic events that happen on battlefields so that people will no longer recognize these places as battlefields can be a positive work since the grass do not want people to dwell on the negative things that have happened. They want people to recover at once and be not affected with the tragedy that have happened. On the contrary, the work of the grass can also be negative since it does not allow people to at least mourn for their deads and they do not want them to have a recollection of their past. The poem ends with the grass repeating its demand to be allowed to work only means that the grass work begins when lives end and it will be repeatedly done over and over again.

3. Road Not Taken

The last two lines of the poem are simply an endorsement of the author's choice. However, it can also be interpreted as less than positive since it can be sensed as a form of regret for not choosing the road that he thinks is best for him.

4. After Apple Picking

The speaker's sleep might be interpreted as a moment of silence and thinking. It is just like having a break after a very tiring work or a long day. Interpreting it this way will make the poem to be more meaningful since it implies that whatever are nature of work are we need to have some sort of rest in order to rearrange things and see if there are still works to be done or accomplished.
