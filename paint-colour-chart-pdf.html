2. In the front of a building there are three doors each to be painted a different colour from twelve different available colours. How many colour arrangements for the doors are there?


We can solve this using the formula for permutation of n objects taken r at a time. Where n is equal to the number of colors and r is equal to the number of doors.
P(n,r) = n!/(n-r)!
P(12,3) = 12!/(12-3)!
P(12,3) = 12x11x10x9!/9!
(Just cancel the 9! from both numerator and denominator for easier solving)
P(12,3) = 12x11x10
P(12,3) = 1320
.
Therefore, there are 1,320 possible arrangements for the doors.

7. Differentiate Colour Gamut from Complementary Colours​


Answer:

Complementary colors are color combinations that, when combined or mixed, cancel each other out (lose hue) and produce a grayscale color such as white or black. They create the greatest contrast for those two hues when put adjacent to each other. Complementary colors are often known as "opposite colors." While the color gamut of a device is defined as the range of colors it can create or record. On the chromaticity diagram, it is typically represented by an enclosed area of the device's primary colors. Monitors, for example, have primary hues of red, green, and blue.

26. Contemporary indo-china and eat indies this colourful and detailed designs , painted fabric called?​


Answer:

Batik

Explanation:

Batik is a traditional wax-resist cloth dyeing of Java, Indonesia. The word itself came from Javanese ambatik from amba (wide) and -tik (to make a dot). This tradition has been around in Java since 7th or 8th century.

There are several techniques to add batik to a piece of cloth. Here are the common methods: Written batik (batik tulis), painted batik (batik lukis), and stamped batik (batik cap).  

Batik has been used in Java for centuries as traditional costume in royal palaces, dance costumes, and special occasions such as birth, wedding, and death ceremonies. The tradition of making and use of batik has reached other countries in Southeast Asia like the Philippines (where the Maranao okir is based) and even as far as sub-Saharan Africa where they were introduced by Dutch and English traders.
