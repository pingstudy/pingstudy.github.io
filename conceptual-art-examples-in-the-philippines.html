12. principle of conceptual art​


Answer:

CONCEPTUAL ART

Conceptual art is art for which the idea (or concept) behind the work is more important than the finished art object. It emerged as an art movement in the 1960s and the term usually refers to art made from the mid-1960s to the mid-1970s.

CONCEPTUAL ARTWORKS

Conceptual art can be – and can look like – almost anything. This is because, unlike a painter or sculptor who will think about how best they can express their idea using paint or sculptural materials and techniques, a conceptual artist uses whatever materials and whatever form is most appropriate to putting their idea across – this could be anything from a performance to a written description. Although there is no one style or form used by conceptual artists, from the late 1960s certain trends emerged.

19. Neodadaism and pop art,conceptual art,op art,the new realism


Answer:

Neo Dadaism— Neo-Dada was a movement with audio, visual and literary manifestations that had similarities in method or intent with earlier Dada artwork. It sought to close the gap between art and daily life, and was a combination of playfulness, iconoclasm, and appropriation.

Pop art— art based on modern popular culture and the mass media, especially as a critical or ironic comment on traditional fine art values.

Conceptual art— art in which the idea or concept presented by the artist is considered more important than its appearance or execution.

Op art— a form of abstract art that gives the illusion of movement by the precise use of pattern and color, or in which conflicting patterns emerge and overlap. Bridget Riley and Victor Vasarely are its most famous exponents.

New realism— a form of realism that was developed at the beginning of the 20th century in opposition to idealism, that emphasizes the distinction between the object and the act of sensation, and that holds the objective world to exist independently of the knowing mind and to be directly knowable.

24. 7. Marilyn Monroe, by Andy Warol (1967) is an example of what art style. A. Op Art B. Pop Art C. Conceptual art D. all of the above


Answer:

Pop Art first emerged in the mid-1950s in both America and Britain. It was in part a reaction against the emotional seriousness and introspection of Abstract Expressionism, which had been affected by the experience of the Second World War. The term ‘Pop Art’ was coined in 1954 by the British-born critic and curator Lawrence Alloway (1926–90) to describe the ‘popular art’ and imagery produced by a mass culture that ‘high’ art had begun to pick up and use: flags, jukeboxes, badges, advertising logos, comic strips and magazines. After the intensity of Abstract Expressionism, Pop Art favoured irony and impersonal techniques in the creation of art works, as well as a return to figurative painting.

28. what is the relevance of conceptual art​


Answer:

Conceptual art emerged in the 1960s and 1970s, and its relevance is primarily based on the emphasis it places on the idea or concept behind the artwork, rather than on its visual appearance. This movement encourages intellectual engagement and critical thinking among viewers by challenging traditional notions of what art should be. Conceptual artists often use unconventional materials and language to express their ideas, which can lead to a deeper appreciation of the creative process and the role of art in society. Conceptual art has also played an important role in promoting new approaches to art-making and challenging established norms in the art world. Overall, the relevance of conceptual art lies in its ability to broaden our understanding of art, encourage critical thinking, and promote new avenues for artistic expression.
