Answer:

Activity A

Floodwater is the most common effect of deforestation and improper waste disposal. Floodwater are hazardous, can put you at risk of infectious diseases, chemical dangers and injury.

Floodwater wreak havoc on the afflicted areas day-to-day operations. Serene floods can sometimes result in massive devastation. Floods claims the lives of a large number of people and animals. A number of people have been hurt. Floods also increase the spread of diseases. The stagnant water attracts mosquitos, which spread diseases like malaria and dengue ferer.

People are also subjected to power onstages due to the risk of electrocution. They also have to deal with exorbitant prices. As the supply of food and goods becomes more limited, prices will naturally rise. This is a significant problem for the average person.

As a student, the least we can do is to practice proper garbage disposal. Through this, we can help lesson the effects of floodwaters. We can also participate in community services like tree-planting.

Activity B

Since the beginning of the pandemic, we've all been hearing the phrase "new normal." But the new normal we've been living and working in for the past six

months is anything but normal.

Work has relocated to our homes, so as studying. Our personal lives have been upended, we have stopped shaking hands with strangers, travel has all but ceased, and regular day-to-day activities have mostly adapted to stay-at-home instructions as well as health and safety restrictions put in place around the world.

Having said that, there have been several positives and fresh realizations as a result of this unforeseen scenario, many of which will carry over into our professional life in the future.

Nobody knows when the "old normal" will return; in fact, I believe the old way of studying and living will vanish. My aim is that after we return to school and resume our normal routines, we will begin to integrate new methods of learning and connecting with our teachers and fellow students. We'll live and study in a new "new normal," which I feel we've been studying toward for a long time.
