    Before the Spaniards arrived in the Philippines, Ivatans constructed a house out of coconut glass. These dwellings were small, well-located, and built to withstand severe winds. The Spaniards taught the Ivatans how to produce lime on a big scale, allowing them to construct the now-famous stone house. The 1 meter thick limestone wall is intended to shield you from the harsh climate of Batanes, often known as the Philippines' Typhoon Terminal Passage. The basic coconut glass is still used as the house's roof and is densely constructed to withstand severe winds. These homes are comparable to the white homes of New Zealand, Ireland and the Scottish Highlands. Pre-colonial residents of Ivatan also built fortified hills protected by steep walls known as ijang (or idjang).
