1. A 30 F capacitor is connected to a 9.0 V battery.  Calculate the charge on the capacitor​


Answer:

the charge on the capacitor is 270 C.

Explanation:

The charge on a capacitor is given by:

Q = CV

where Q is the charge on the capacitor, C is the capacitance of the capacitor, and V is the voltage across the capacitor.

Given:

C = 30 F

V = 9.0 V

Using the formula, we get:

Q = CV

Q = (30 F)(9.0 V)

Q = 270 coulombs (C)

Therefore, the charge on the capacitor is 270 C.

3. The figure shows five capacitors connected to a 6.0V battery.a. determine the equivalent capacitanceb. calculate the total energy stored in the capacitors


Answer:

a. The equivalent capacitance is 3.5μF

b. The total energy stored is 63 μJ

Explanation:

Here, we are to solve for the total capacitance and energy stored in the capacitors. To do this, we will solve this per group.

For the first group composed of 3.0μF and 6.0μF, let us solve for the total capacitance. Since they are in series, we can use the formula:

C₃₆ = [1/3μF + 1/6μF] ⁻¹

C₃₆ = 2μF

For the second group composed of three (3) 4.5μF, connected also in series, let us solve for the total capacitance, we have:

C₃₍₄₅₎ = [1/4.5μF + 1/4.5μF + 1/4.5μF] ⁻¹

C₃₍₄₅₎ = 1.5μF

a. Now that we have simplified these two (2) groups, let us solve for the total capacitance. Since they are in parallel, let us add them to solve for the total capacitance, we have:

Ceq = C₃₆ + C₃₍₄₅₎

Ceq = 2μF +  1.5μF

Ceq = 3.5μF

b. Now, let us solve for the total energy stored, we have:

U = ¹/₂CeqV²

U = ¹/₂(3.5 x 10⁻⁶)(6)²

U = 63 x 10⁻⁶ J

U = 63 μJ

Therefore, the total capacitance is 3.5μF and the total energy stored is 63μJ.

5. Three capacitors C1, C2, and C3 have capacitances of 3 F, 2F, and 5 F, respectively. They are connected in a circuit as shown. Calculate: a. The equivalent capacitance b. The potential difference across each capacitor c. The charge on each capacitor.


Answer:

a. The equivalent capacitance is 2.5F

b. The potential difference across each capacitor is 1.5 V

c. The charge on each capacitor is 4.5C, 3C and 7.5C respectively

Explanation:

Here is a series - parallel connection of capacitors. To find for the following requirements, we are also to combine the rules of series and parallel connections.

a. To solve for the equivalent capacitance, we add C1 and C2 because they are in parallel. Then we get the reciprocal of the individual reciprocals of the sum of C1 and C2 and C3.

Ceq = [1/(C1 + C2) + 1/C3]⁻¹

Substituting the given data:

Ceq = [1/(3F + 2F) + 1/5F]⁻¹  

Ceq = 2.5F

b. Now, let us solve for the potential difference across each capacitor.

Let us solve first the total charge using the formula Q = CV

Qeq = CeqVeq

Qeq = (2.5F)(3V) = 7.5C

Therefore the potential difference across C₃ is

V₃ = Qeq/C₃ = 7.5C / 5F = 1.5V

Since the circuit will be equivalent to a series form after combining C₁ and C₂, The total voltage for the whole circuit will be:

Vt = V₁₂ + V₃

Here, we can solve for the potential difference for C₁ and C₂  

3V = V₁₂ + 1.5V

V₁₂ = 3 - 1.5 = 1.5 V

Therefore, the potential difference across C₁ and C₂ is 1.5V.

c. Now, let us solve for the charge on each capacitor. Let us use again the formula, Q = CV

Q₁ = C₁V₁ = (3F)(1.5V) = 4.5C

Q₂ = C₂V₂ = (2F)(1.5V) = 3C

Q₃ = C₃V₃ = (5F)(1.5V) = 7.5C

Parallel is your only reliable option.

You can increase the total energy either by increasing voltage or current. Increasing voltage would mean connecting many capacitors in series while increasing the current would mean connecting many capacitors in parallel.

The connection is parallel is very simple to understand. They all share the same voltage and each capacitor you add increase the energy in direct proportion of the capacitance value in farad. If you want 10 times the energy compared to a single capacitor, you connect 10 capacitors in parallel.

Connecting capacitors in series in the hope to increase the voltage is risky. Even if they are identical, one will charge or discharge faster and you end up destroying the weaker by exceeding the voltage or reversing it’s polarity.

Because doubling the voltage increase the total energy by 4, you may think it worth the risk to connect a few capacitors in series. But the capacitance of capacitors in series decrease by the same proportion so the math revert to the same ; each capacitor in series adds to the total power in a linear not square ratio.

9. Consider that the battery you used has a voltage of 50 V and that two capacitors of capacitance of 1 = 1.0 2 = 2.0 are connected in parallel. Calculate: a. the equivalent capacitance, and b. the charge on each capacitor.


Answer:

a. The equivalent capacitance is 3μF

b. The charge on each capacitor is 50μC and 0.1mC respectively

Explanation:

Here we have a parallel connection of capacitors. For the rules of parallel connections:  

a. The total or equivalent capacitance is added

b. The voltage across each capacitor is the same

Now, let us solve.

a. For the equivalent capacitance, we use the formula

Ceq = C₁ + C₂

Ceq = 1μF + 2μF

Ceq = 3μF

b. Now, let us solve for the charge on each capacitor using the formula

Q = CV

Q₁ = C₁V₁ = (1μF)(50V) = 50 x 10 ⁻⁶ C = 50μC

Q₂ = C₂V₂ = (2μF)(50V) = 0.1 X 10⁻³ = 0.1mC

Therefore, the equivalent capacitance is 3μF and charge on each capacitor are 50μC and 0.1mC respectively.

18. Two capacitors of capacitance 4.0 F and 6.0 F are charged to a potential difference of 12.0 V and 6.0 V, respectively. If the capacitors were arranged in a parallel connection, calculate the total energy stored.


Answer:

The total energy stored is 388.8 J

Explanation:

Here we are to solve for the total energy stored in the capacitors.

Reminder: Capacitors connected in parallel have the same value for voltages but since each capacitor have different potential difference across them, therefore, the capacitors are connected in series.

So, we will be using series circuit of capacitors instead of parallel.

For the formula, we will be using:

U = ¹/₂CV²      

where

U     is the stored energy, unit is in Joules, J

C     is the capacitance, unit is in Farad, F

V     is the potential difference across each capacitor, unit is in volts, V

For the given information

C1 = 4.0 F

C2 = 6.0 F

V1 = 12.0 V

V2 = 6.0 V

U = ?

Solving the problem

1. Let us solve first for the total capacitance using the formula

Ceq = [1/C1 + 1/C2] ⁻¹

And substitute the given capacitors, we have:

Ceq = [1/C1 + 1/C2] ⁻¹

Ceq = [1/4 + 1/6] ⁻¹

Ceq = 2.4 F

2. Now, let us also solve for the total voltage using the formula

V = V1 + V2

And substitute the given potential difference, we have:

V = 12V + 6V

V = 18 V

3. Now, we are ready to solve for the total energy stored using the formula

U = ¹/₂CeqV²      

Substitute the given information, we have:

U = ¹/₂(2.4)(18)²      

U = 388.8 J

Therefore, the total energy stored in the capacitors is 388.88 J.

23. 4. How to test a Capacitor:​


Answer:

28. Three capacitors C1, C2, and C3 have capacitances of 3 μF, 2μF, and 5 μF, respectively. They are connected in a circuit as shown. Calculate: The equivalent capacitance The potential difference across each capacitor The charge on each capacitor.


Answer:

a. The equivalent capacitance is 2.5μF

b. The potential difference across each capacitor is 1.5 V.

c. The charge on each capacitor is 4.5μC, 3μC and 7.5μC respectively

Explanation:

Here is a series - parallel connection of capacitors. To find for the following requirements, we are also to combine the rules of series and parallel connections.

a. To solve for the equivalent capacitance, we add C1 and C2 because they are in parallel. Then we get the reciprocal of the individual reciprocals of the sum of C1 and C2 and C3.  

Ceq = [1/(C1 + C2) + 1/C3]⁻¹

Substituting the given data:

Ceq = [1/(3μF + 2μF) + 1/5μF]⁻¹  

Ceq = 2.5μF

b. Now, let us solve for the potential difference across each capacitor.  

Let us solve first the total charge using the formula Q = CV  

Qeq = CeqVeq  

Qeq = (2.5μF)(3V) = 7.5μC

Therefore the potential difference across C₃ is

V₃ = Qeq/C₃ = 7.5μC / 5μF = 1.5V

Since the circuit will be equivalent to a series form after combining C₁ and C₂, The total voltage for the whole circuit will be:

Vt = V₁₂ + V₃

Here, we can solve for the potential difference for C₁ and C₂

3V = V₁₂ + 1.5V

V₁₂ = 3 - 1.5 = 1.5 V  

Therefore, the potential difference across C₁ and C₂ is 1.5V

c. Now, let us solve for the charge on each capacitor. Let us use again the formula, Q = CV

Q₁ = C₁V₁ = (3μF)(1.5V) = 4.5μC  

Q₂ = C₂V₂ = (2μF)(1.5V) = 3μC  

Q₃ = C₃V₃ = (5μF)(1.5V) = 7.5μC

30. Procedures on how to test a capacitor? ​


Answer:

1. Disconnect the suspected capacitor from the power supply or make sure at least one lead of the capacitor is disconnected.

2. Make sure that the capacitor is fully discharged.

3. Connect two separate leads to the capacitor terminals. (Optional)

4. Now safely connect these leads to 230 V AC Supply for a very short period (about 1-4 Sec) [or for a short time where the Voltage rise to 63.2% of the Source Voltage] .

5. Remove safety leads from the 230 V AC Supply.

6. Now short the capacitor terminals (Please be careful to do that and make sure that you have wear safety goggles)

7. If it makes a strong spark, then the capacitor is good.

8. If it makes a weak spark, then it is a bad capacitor and change it immediately with a new one.
